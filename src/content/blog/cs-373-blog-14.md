## Part 1: Looking Back

### How well do you feel the course actually conveyed these ideas?

The course conveyed ideas like Test-Driven Development and System Design fairly well. I think it was mostly focused, during the lectures at least, on Python language features, which is useful for technical interviews but not as broad as the syllabus suggests. However, the takeaways were definetly emphasized in the lecture content and the notes.

I think AI-assisted tests is a great tool for easily setting up a TDD environment before you get started writing code. It was helpful for me for the IDB projects and I think I'll carry it forward for future projects.

### Compared to your other CS classes at UT, how much did you grow in this course?

In all truth, probably a lot less than the other classes. I think this class repeats a lot of topics from Data Structures, a prerequisite for this class. It does help to go over some Python language specifics and some system design concepts that are additional to the stuff from 314, especially for preperation for technical interviews, but you definetly will not learn as much as you do in classes like Computer Architecture or Operating Systems. Those classes show you an entire different perspective on computing and CS, while this class is more focused on honing your 314 skills. Useful, but not as much growth.

## Part 2: My Experiences

### Underserved community requirement on IDB

I think this requirement had good intention behind it, but it ended up leading a lot of the groups in the class to make very similar projects. I don't think there was as much breadth of projects as there could have been had we not been restricted to a specific local community. Or perhaps you could tell students that they can't do Austin or Texas to ensure people pick diverse areas.

### 1 Project on HackerRank

I actually really enjoyed this project. I think the class would benefit from more individual HackerRank projects. They're a good way to practice some larger scale programming problems that you might encounter in a real-world interview.

### Rubrics on Canvas, class website, and Google Docs

There was plenty of discrepancy between the rubrics on Canvas, the class website and Google Docs. In all honesty, it seems like a bad idea to have three different sources of truth for the rubrics, especially as they often varied in specific details on project requirements. It was also difficult to know exactly what was expected of us for each project phase, as the rubrics carried over some information from previous phases and even specified some information that we didn't have to do for the current phase, but was required for a future phase.

It was especially difficult when Dr. Downing would stipulate requirements in class that *directly* contradicted the rubrics on the class website and Google Docs. It added a fourth source of truth that was impossible to keep track of and follow, and was especially frusturating when he would become angry at the students for not following his instructions that he had directly contradicted himself.

### TA office hours

TA office hours were good and fairly helpful. It was a little tough when the hours kept changing all the time, but they were helpful and accommodating and pretty available.

### 14 Collaborative Papers on Perusall

I thought the required papers were not super related to what we were learning in class, and had more to do with object-oriented development and design patterns. I also didn't love how 90% of the papers were dated before 2005, over 20 years ago. A lot has changed since then in the world of software engineering and development, so it'd be nice to also mix in some more recent papers.
